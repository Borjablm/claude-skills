# Brand Profile: ecomm.design

**Style reference:** Glen Allsopp on [detailed.com](https://detailed.com/affiliate-serps/) — data-led, conversational, methodologically transparent, structurally minimal. Adapted from the ai-tutor.ai profile, retuned for ecommerce founders, designers, and agency clients.

## About
- **Site:** ecomm.design — ecommerce website design gallery (4,118 stores classified by platform, category, technology, and traffic) plus Shopify development, theme design, app development, and migration services.
- **Position:** The reference catalogue for design-led ecommerce. Most "best of" ecomm content is affiliate slop or vendor marketing. Our angle is data-led and operator-flavoured.
- **Audience to convert:** Founders evaluating platforms, designers researching patterns, agencies sending clients here, and Shopify-curious teams sizing up a migration.

## Voice & Tone

**Persona:** Curious operator looking at the gallery's own data, not authority figure. We share what we found by digging into our own dataset, with the reader looking over our shoulder. Confident enough to commit to claims, honest about evidence limits.

**Core qualities:**
- **Data-led from the first sentence.** Open with a specific number, ranking, or finding. Never with throat-clearing about "today's ecommerce landscape."
- **Conversational confidence.** Direct, plain language. We are talking to one founder or one designer over coffee, not lecturing a conference room.
- **Methodologically transparent.** Show how we got the numbers. Mention sample sizes, time windows, sources. Explain what's in scope and what isn't.
- **Curious, not certain.** Surprise allowed. "I don't know about you, but that number was higher than we expected." Vulnerability is a strength.
- **Operator-flavoured.** We've built ecommerce stores. We talk like people who've shipped platforms, not like consultants. Specific tool names, concrete scenarios, real migrations.

## Writing Patterns to Mimic

### 1. Data-driven openings
Open with a sharp, specific number that creates curiosity. The headline finding goes first. Context follows.

**Good:** "Klaviyo basically requires Shopify. 52% of Shopify stores in our gallery use it. On WooCommerce, that drops to 7%."

**Bad:** "Choosing the right ecommerce platform is one of the most important decisions for any DTC brand."

### 2. The "to put that in perspective" pattern
Never let a number stand alone. Always contextualize.

- "(or, 23% of the gallery)"
- "To put that in perspective, that's almost double the second-largest platform."
- "Add menswear, dresses, and outwear and you've described almost a quarter of the platform's gallery presence."

### 3. Methodology transparency
Tell the reader how you got the numbers. This is not academic dryness; it's trust-building.

**Good:** "We pulled 4,118 entries from our gallery via the WordPress REST API in late April 2026. 99% had platform tagged, 99% had at least one tool. The data reflects the stack at the moment each store was added."

### 4. Forward-momentum section endings
End sections with a question or hook the next section answers. No summary paragraphs.

**Good:** "But what does platform choice actually lock you into? That's where the tooling data gets specific."

**Bad:** "In summary, the platform you choose has implications for your tooling."

### 5. Punchy single-line paragraphs
After a data reveal, drop a one-line paragraph for emphasis.

> 23% of the gallery is "Custom" or "Unknown."
>
> When we first saw that number, we assumed: indie brands, hand-rolled builds, designers showing off bespoke work.
>
> We were wrong.

### 6. Parenthetical asides for context
Embed context inline rather than breaking flow with footnotes or sidebars.

- "Yotpo (the heavyweight reviews platform, not the SMS layer they sell separately) shows up on 18% of Shopify stores."
- "Most of these tools (78% of them, in fact) cluster around analytics, advertising, and payments."

### 7. Personal vulnerability at the close
Section endings or article closes can shift to a more personal note. Honesty about doubt or surprise lands harder than another stat.

**Good close:** "We've been curating this gallery for ten years and we still get surprised by what the dataset shows. The pattern most people assume — that platform is just plumbing — doesn't hold."

## Pronouns
- **"We"** — for our perspective, our analysis, our team. Used throughout.
- **"You"** — direct address to the reader (founders, designers, agency leads).
- **"I"** — sparingly, for personal/vulnerable moments. One or two uses per article max.

## Audience Context
- **Primary:** Ecommerce founders, designers researching patterns, agency leads scoping platform migrations or builds.
- **Mid-senior** with budget authority or technical authority. Smart, skeptical, time-poor.
- **Pain points:** Pressure to ship; vendor pitches everywhere; fear of locking into the wrong platform; opaque tooling cost; wanting evidence not anecdotes.
- **Reads our content for:** Honest answers to "which platform fits my brand?" — not for inspiration or affiliate roundups.

## Structure & Formatting

**Default to prose. Use structure sparingly.**

### Headings
- H1 for title (only once)
- H2 for major sections — phrased as **declarative findings**, not categories. "Klaviyo lives on Shopify. Mailchimp lives everywhere else." beats "Email Marketing Tools."
- H3 sparingly, for sub-points within long sections
- **Avoid stuffy headings** like "Methodology," "Conclusion," "Findings." Prefer "How we ran the numbers" or "What this means for next month's redesign."

### Lists
- **Bullet lists for enumeration only.** Don't bury narrative in bullets.
- **Numbered lists for rankings.**
- Most prose should stay as prose, not be flattened into bullets.

### Charts & Visuals
- **Charts over tables** for any data with more than two columns of meaning.
- **200–400 words of prose between visuals.** Don't pile up charts.
- **Brief captions or inline reference,** not full figure-caption ceremony.
- **Real screenshots from the gallery** when illustrating a finding ("here's what an enterprise programmatic stack looks like in the wild" → screenshot of an actual Custom/Unknown entry).

### Tables
- Use tables only when the data is genuinely tabular and direct comparison is the point.
- Skip tables when prose with bolded numbers communicates the same thing more naturally.

### Bold and emphasis
- **Bold for load-bearing 2–4 words inline** ("**enterprise programmatic ad-tech footprint**")
- *Italics for short emphasis* ("the *vast majority*")
- Never bold whole sentences.

### Callout boxes
- **Use sparingly.** A Key Takeaways box near the top is fine. Mid-article callouts only when the insight genuinely deserves visual interruption.

## Vocabulary

**Use freely:** stack, archetype, platform, lock-in, migration, gallery, signature, footprint, pattern, the data shows, in practice, we found, we ran the numbers, to put that in perspective, design-led, mid-market, enterprise, headless

**Use carefully:** disrupt, transform (only when precise), AI-powered (rarely relevant here)

**Never use:** revolutionary, game-changer, "unlock potential", em-dashes for dramatic effect, "not only... but also", "leverage" as a verb, breathless claims about ecommerce being "transformed," "in today's rapidly evolving ecommerce landscape," "let's dive in"

## Voice Examples

**Good (Allsopp-style applied to ecommerce):**
> We pulled the tech stacks of all 4,118 ecommerce stores in our gallery to figure out how design-led brands actually build their stores in 2026.
>
> Three findings stood out. First, Shopify owns 46% of the gallery — but the label hides three sub-stories. Second, the platform you pick predicts what kind of brand you're allowed to be. Third, switching platforms resets your tool world.
>
> Here's the data, the methodology, and what it means if you're picking a platform or planning a migration.

**Bad (the corporate "thought leader" voice we are not):**
> In today's rapidly evolving ecommerce landscape, choosing the right platform is more important than ever. Forward-thinking DTC brands need to leverage cutting-edge solutions to unlock their full potential and transform their customer experience.

**Good (methodology transparency):**
> We pulled 4,118 entries via the WordPress REST API on April 28, 2026. Platform was tagged on 99% of entries; at least one tool on 99.4%. Each entry's stack reflects what we captured at the moment the store was added to the gallery, not necessarily today's stack. Older entries are older snapshots.

**Bad (over-confident assertion):**
> Our proprietary research has revealed exclusive insights into the ecommerce tooling landscape.

**Good (operator-flavoured):**
> If you're moving from Shopify to Magento Enterprise, the email tool of choice changes the same week. Klaviyo's Shopify-only by practical reality. You'll be on Mailchimp or Bronto by Tuesday.

**Good (personal vulnerability close):**
> I've been looking at this gallery for years, and the dataset still surprises us. The "Shopify is just for small DTC" narrative doesn't survive contact with the data. Neither does "Magento is dying." Both are doing real work, just for different brands.

## Anti-Patterns (Never Do This)
- Hype framing about ecommerce being "revolutionized" by AI / headless / anything
- Generic "ecommerce in 2026" essays without specific data
- Self-promotional product references in article body (CTA at the end is fine; mid-article is not)
- Affiliate-disclosure language that drowns the prose
- Vague claims about conversion lift without sources
- Em-dashes for dramatic effect, "unlock", "leverage", "revolutionize"
- Talking down to readers as if ecommerce is new to them
- Long takeaway boxes substituting for clear writing
- Tables when prose works better
- Stuffy academic tone ("In conclusion," "It is hypothesized that")
- Throat-clearing intros that take 3 paragraphs to get to the point
- Hiding behind passive voice ("It was found that")
- Burying the lead — every section opens with the finding, not the setup

## Authorship
Default: write as the ecomm.design team ("we"). One or two uses of "I" per article are allowed and encouraged at vulnerable moments — surprise, doubt, personal stake. The reader trusts a person more than a brand.
